# Quiet Hours — Chapter 1 Draft v0.2

## The Broken Lamp

Mara carried the lamp in a plain cotton bag so no one would ask about it.

The bag was blue once, but too many washes had turned it the colour of old rain. The lamp base pressed against her hip as she crossed the community building foyer, careful not to let the plug swing loose against her leg.

The main hallway was already louder than it should have been.

A trolley squeaked near the noticeboards. Someone laughed outside the office. The strip lights hummed above her with their thin, needling sound.

Mara kept her eyes on the repair desk sign.

REPAIRS AND SMALL ELECTRICALS.

Underneath, in smaller letters:

ASK FOR ELLIS.

She had seen him before. Most people in the building had. He noticed loose hinges, flickering bulbs, chairs that scraped, labels peeling at the corners.

Once, she had watched him lower a noticeboard without making the receptionist explain why the height was wrong.

That made him easier to approach than most people.

Not easy.

Easier.

There was no queue at the repair desk. Mara stopped at the edge of the counter, set the bag down, and took one second to check whether she had enough words.

Ellis looked up from a small radio with its back removed.

"Morning," he said.

It was a normal word. It did not ask much.

"Morning," Mara said.

She opened the bag and folded the cloth back from the lamp.

For a moment the small brass base caught the light. It had a green shade, slightly faded at the rim, and a pull chain with a tiny acorn-shaped end. Mara put two fingers over the chain before it could move.

Ellis did not reach for it straight away.

"What is it doing?" he asked.

Good question. Practical. Narrow.

"Flickers," Mara said. "Then stops. The switch feels loose. The plug is old."

Ellis nodded once and wrote something on a repair pad.

He did not ask where it came from.

That helped.

He turned the lamp slightly, checking the cable without pulling it hard. His hands were steady. He looked at the plug, then the switch, then the shade, as if each part deserved its own answer.

Mara watched the movement and kept her own hands still on the edge of the counter.

"I can check the plug and the switch," he said. "The cable may need replacing. I won't know until I open it."

She nodded.

There was a pause where another person might have filled the space. A joke. A story. A question about whether the lamp was special.

Ellis picked up a small card instead.

"I can write the collection time down if that's easier."

Mara looked at him then.

Not long.

Just enough to check whether he had made the offer loudly, like a performance of kindness, or quietly, like a tool placed within reach.

He was already writing.

Lamp: plug / switch / cable check.  
Ready: Thursday after 2.  
Ask at repair desk.  
No need to wait.

He turned the card round so she could read it.

The words were square and plain. No arrows. No extra explanation. No looping speech she would have to hold in her head until she could get away and sort it.

"Thank you," Mara said.

"That's all right."

He put a small paper tag around the cable. He did it carefully, not too tight.

The acorn on the pull chain tapped once against the brass base.

Mara felt the sound in the centre of her chest.

She picked up the card before she could put a hand over the chain again.

"Thursday," she said, because repeating one word was easier than explaining why the lamp mattered.

"After two," Ellis said.

Then he stopped.

No extra sentence came after it.

Mara put the card into the back pocket of her notebook. She folded the cotton bag and held it against her side, smaller now without the lamp inside it.

For one second she did not know what to do with her empty hand.

Ellis looked back at the repair pad.

That helped too.

It gave her somewhere to leave from.

Mara nodded, though he was not looking directly at her, and stepped away from the counter.

The hallway noise returned as soon as she left the repair desk: trolley wheel, office laugh, strip-light hum.

But she had the card.

Thursday after 2.

No need to wait.

She read the last line once more before she went back to the library.

It should have been nothing.

A repair ticket.

A practical note.

A lamp left with someone who knew what to do with loose switches and old plugs.

Still, as Mara slid the card into her notebook again, she felt the small clean shape of the words stay with her.

No need to wait.
