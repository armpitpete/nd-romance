# ND Readable Romance Checklist v1.0

## Purpose

Use this checklist to test whether a romance is **emotionally legible, structurally trackable, and readable without social fog**.

This is not a formula for bland writing.  
The goal is:

- clarity without flattening
- depth without muddle
- desire without confusion
- conflict without needless vagueness

---

# Core rule

A romance is ND-readable when the reader can track:

- who feels what
- why they feel it
- what each scene changes
- what each person thinks is happening
- what is actually happening
- what makes the bond matter

The reader may be uncertain about the outcome.  
The reader should **not** be uncertain about the emotional logic.

---

# Non-goals

This checklist does **not** require:

- over-explaining every feeling
- removing all subtext
- making characters unnaturally explicit
- reducing complexity
- writing diagnosis-first characters

This checklist **does** require:

- anchored emotional cause and effect
- clear scene jobs
- visible relationship progression
- distinguishable states
- readable repair

---

# Pass condition

The romance is working if an ND reader can follow the bond, the strain, and the movement of the relationship **without having to guess wildly**.

---

# Part 1: Bond foundation

## 1. Why these two?

- [ ] It is clear why these two people are drawn to each other specifically.
- [ ] The attraction is not generic or transferable.
- [ ] The bond is based on more than proximity, convenience, or author insistence.
- [ ] There is a readable answer to: **Why this person, and why now?**

## 2. Specific attraction

- [ ] Attraction is shown through concrete noticing.
- [ ] Each person sees something particular in the other.
- [ ] Chemistry is observable in scene, not just declared.
- [ ] The story can show attraction without relying only on kisses, sex, or summary language.

## 3. Why the bond matters

- [ ] The story makes clear what is emotionally at stake.
- [ ] The reader understands what each person stands to gain or lose.
- [ ] The relationship changes something real in each character’s life or self-understanding.
- [ ] The bond feels meaningful before the ending confirms it.

---

# Part 2: Character readability

## 4. Lead clarity

For each lead:

- [ ] Their want is clear.
- [ ] Their fear is clear.
- [ ] Their wound or defended place is clear.
- [ ] Their false belief about love, trust, or self-worth is clear enough to track.
- [ ] Their behaviour follows readable emotional logic.

## 5. Distinguishable states

- [ ] Overload, fear, anger, embarrassment, desire, shame, and withdrawal do not all read the same.
- [ ] Silence has readable meaning in context.
- [ ] Distance is not automatically coded as rejection.
- [ ] Closeness is not automatically coded as safety.
- [ ] The prose helps the reader tell one state from another.

## 6. Masking and legibility

- [ ] If masking matters, the story shows what is masked and at what cost.
- [ ] The reader can tell the difference between performed ease and real ease.
- [ ] There are moments where one character becomes more legible to the other.
- [ ] Love is not defined as rewarding better performance of normality.

---

# Part 3: Scene construction

## 7. Every romance scene has a job

For each major scene, identify one primary job:

- [ ] first noticing
- [ ] first ease
- [ ] increased trust
- [ ] sharper attraction
- [ ] misread
- [ ] vulnerability
- [ ] rupture
- [ ] repair
- [ ] clearer choice
- [ ] proof of care
- [ ] proof of incompatibility
- [ ] unmasking
- [ ] commitment test

If the scene has no clear job:

- [ ] cut it
- [ ] merge it
- [ ] rewrite it

## 8. Scene-end change

For each major scene:

- [ ] Something changes by the end.
- [ ] The change is trackable.
- [ ] The reader can say what is now true that was not true before.
- [ ] Emotional movement happens on-page, not only in summary afterward.

## 9. Point-of-view clarity

- [ ] The reader can tell what the point-of-view character believes is happening.
- [ ] The story gives enough evidence to judge whether that belief is correct.
- [ ] Misreadings are precise, not mushy.
- [ ] Internal thought does not blur the actual scene action.

---

# Part 4: Communication readability

## 10. Dialogue meaning

- [ ] Dialogue has readable intent.
- [ ] Important lines do not rely on tone alone.
- [ ] When a line is ambiguous, the ambiguity is purposeful and anchored.
- [ ] The reader can usually tell whether a line is care, defence, evasion, flirtation, pressure, or refusal.

## 11. Conflict quality

- [ ] Conflict does not exist only because nobody will say one clear sentence.
- [ ] Miscommunication arises from believable difference, fear, habit, timing, or pressure.
- [ ] The conflict reveals character rather than just delaying the plot.
- [ ] The conflict could not be fully solved by one unrealistic five-second conversation.

## 12. Clarification and repair

- [ ] At least some important misunderstandings are clarified on-page.
- [ ] Repair is visible, not assumed.
- [ ] Characters can return after rupture without the story treating repair as unromantic.
- [ ] Apology, adjustment, or re-understanding has real weight in the relationship.

---

# Part 5: Sensory and regulation readability

## 13. Condition of the scene

- [ ] The reader can tell when a character is regulated, strained, overloaded, shut down, dissociated, or avoidant.
- [ ] Sensory environment is present where relevant.
- [ ] The story distinguishes social difficulty from sensory difficulty when needed.
- [ ] Physical closeness is handled with readable consent and condition.

## 14. Care as part of intimacy

- [ ] Care is not written as pity.
- [ ] Care includes precision, not just niceness.
- [ ] The story shows characters learning what helps and what harms.
- [ ] Acts of care are specific enough to feel intimate.
- [ ] Care does not erase desire.

## 15. Rhythm compatibility

- [ ] Differences in timing, response speed, routine, recovery, and contact needs are visible.
- [ ] These differences affect the romance structure in meaningful ways.
- [ ] The story explores whether the two can build a shared rhythm.
- [ ] Compatibility is tested through pattern, not just declaration.

---

# Part 6: Structure and progression

## 16. Relationship progression is trackable

- [ ] The romance moves through readable stages.
- [ ] Trust, reliance, vulnerability, and attachment develop in sequence or deliberate counter-sequence.
- [ ] The relationship does not leap ahead without enough on-page earning.
- [ ] The reader can locate the turning points.

## 17. Rising stakes

- [ ] Emotional stakes increase over time.
- [ ] The cost of failure becomes clearer as the relationship develops.
- [ ] The story does not stall after the first spark.
- [ ] Later scenes ask more of the characters than earlier ones.

## 18. Earned turning points

Major beats such as confession, kiss, sex, commitment, break, reunion, or refusal should satisfy all of these:

- [ ] built toward
- [ ] emotionally legible
- [ ] supported by prior scenes
- [ ] not dependent on sudden unexplained reversal
- [ ] consequential afterward

---

# Part 7: Prose-level readability

## 19. Sentence clarity

- [ ] Pronoun reference is clean.
- [ ] Cause and effect are visible.
- [ ] Important actions are not buried in over-decoration.
- [ ] Abstract feeling language is balanced by concrete behaviour.
- [ ] Emotional beats are separated enough to read clearly.

## 20. Subtext control

- [ ] Subtext adds pressure, not confusion.
- [ ] The reader is not forced to infer the entire relationship from implication alone.
- [ ] The prose does not hide the key movement out of fear of being “too obvious.”
- [ ] Mystery is used for tension, not to cover weak construction.

## 21. ND readability sentence test

In crucial scenes:

- [ ] Can a reader tell what just happened?
- [ ] Can a reader tell why it landed that way?
- [ ] Can a reader tell what each person will carry forward from the moment?

If not, rewrite.

---

# Part 8: Red-flag failures

If these are present, the romance likely needs revision:

- [ ] attraction appears without observable basis
- [ ] the leads are emotionally generic
- [ ] important shifts happen off-page
- [ ] conflict depends mostly on avoidable vagueness
- [ ] overload, withdrawal, and rejection are treated as interchangeable
- [ ] care is written as management of a burden
- [ ] one person exists mainly to interpret the other for the reader
- [ ] diagnosis replaces character
- [ ] the romance rewards becoming easier to handle rather than becoming more known
- [ ] the ending resolves the plot without proving relational change

---

# Part 9: Drafting check

Use during early drafting.

## Minimum drafting checks

- [ ] I know why these two matter to each other.
- [ ] I know the main obstacle.
- [ ] I know what each lead misreads.
- [ ] I know what each lead needs but struggles to ask for.
- [ ] I know the scene job before drafting a romance scene.
- [ ] I know what changes by the end of the scene.
- [ ] I can name the current stage of the relationship.

## Drafting danger signs

Stop and fix if:

- [ ] I am relying on “chemistry” instead of specifics.
- [ ] I am hiding character logic to fake intrigue.
- [ ] I do not know whether a scene is intimacy, avoidance, or conflict.
- [ ] I am using banter to avoid deeper movement.
- [ ] I am writing reaction without clear trigger.

---

# Part 10: Revision pass

Use during line and structural revision.

## Structural revision

- [ ] Cut scenes with no relationship job.
- [ ] Merge repetitive beats.
- [ ] Add missing bridge scenes where trust or rupture jumps too far.
- [ ] Sharpen the key turning points.
- [ ] Make the bond legible earlier if needed.

## Scene revision

For each important scene, answer:

- [ ] What does person A think is happening?
- [ ] What does person B think is happening?
- [ ] What is actually happening?
- [ ] What gets noticed?
- [ ] What gets missed?
- [ ] What changes by the end?
- [ ] What new pressure does this create?

## Line revision

- [ ] Replace vague summary with concrete scene evidence where needed.
- [ ] Clarify emotional cause and effect.
- [ ] Remove fake-mysterious dialogue that obscures meaning.
- [ ] Distinguish states that are currently blurred.
- [ ] Make care more precise and less generic.

---

# Part 11: Final proof test

A final ND readability test passes if the answer is yes to all of these:

- [ ] I can explain the bond in plain language.
- [ ] I can explain the conflict in plain language.
- [ ] I can explain the progression in plain language.
- [ ] I can point to scenes that earn the major turns.
- [ ] I can distinguish care from pressure in the text.
- [ ] I can distinguish overload from rejection in the text.
- [ ] I can show how both characters change through the relationship.
- [ ] I can show why the ending is earned.

---

# One-line lock

**The reader should not have to guess the foundation of the bond.**

They may fear failure.  
They may not know the ending.  
But they should be able to follow the emotional truth.
