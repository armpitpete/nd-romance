# ND Readable Romance Draft Failure Triage Sheet v1.0

## Core use

Use this file when a romance draft is **not working**, but the failure is still too vague.

This sheet is for turning:

- “something is off”
- “the middle drags”
- “they do not feel real”
- “the breakup feels fake”
- “the ending lands weakly”

into a clear diagnosis.

This sheet is designed to identify failure in these areas:

- thin bond
- flat chemistry
- false conflict
- dead middle
- unearned rupture
- weak repair
- soft ending
- point-of-view fog
- sensory or processing falseness
- repetition without progression

---

# Fast rule

Do not revise by instinct alone if the draft feels wrong.

First identify:

1. **what is failing**
2. **where it starts failing**
3. **what type of repair the draft needs**
4. **whether the problem is local or structural**

A local problem can be fixed in scenes.  
A structural problem needs chapter or beat rework.

---

# How to use this sheet

## Step 1
Read the draft or current outline and mark the first point where interest, clarity, or emotional belief weakens.

## Step 2
Use the symptom list below to identify the dominant failure mode.

## Step 3
Decide whether the failure is:
- **scene-level**
- **chapter-level**
- **act-level**
- **whole-book**

## Step 4
Choose the matching repair action.

# Primary failure modes

## 1. Thin bond

### What it feels like
The characters may be present on the page together, but the relationship does not feel necessary, specific, or valuable.

### Common symptoms
- attraction exists but attachment does not
- the reader cannot say why these two belong together
- scenes show contact but not meaningful connection
- one or both characters could be replaced without much changing
- the romance feels generic
- the relationship matters because the genre says it should

### Likely causes
- not enough shared pattern
- not enough mutual noticing
- care is absent or too delayed
- connection depends too much on appearance or banter
- no real moments of being understood
- no chapter proves why this bond is different from any other option

### Diagnostic questions
- What does each person see in the other that others do not?
- What exact scenes prove attachment rather than attraction?
- What has each person learned about the other?
- What would each person lose specifically if this relationship failed?

### Repair actions
- add or strengthen care scenes
- add scenes of correct noticing
- give the pair a repeated pattern or shared task
- cut generic flirtation and replace with specific bond evidence
- strengthen the reasons they matter to one another

### Severity marker
- **Yellow:** connection exists but is under-evidenced
- **Red:** there is no believable central bond

---

## 2. Flat chemistry

### What it feels like
The romance is structurally present, but there is little charge.

### Common symptoms
- scenes are polite but inert
- characters speak but do not pull toward or resist each other in a vivid way
- intimacy feels procedural
- attraction is told, not felt
- warmth exists but desire does not
- the draft feels worthy rather than alive

### Likely causes
- no contrast in rhythm, temperament, or need
- dialogue too neutral
- all interaction is emotionally safe too early
- fear, longing, curiosity, or tension is underwritten
- the prose does not register bodily or emotional charge
- the characters never surprise each other

### Diagnostic questions
- What is the source of pull between them?
- What does being near each other change in the room?
- What is charged in the interaction: humour, restraint, gentleness, frustration, relief, admiration?
- Where does desire become visible?

### Repair actions
- sharpen contrast between them
- increase scene-level tension without using nonsense conflict
- add moments of interrupted intimacy, near-speech, held attention, or restraint
- make attraction specific
- ensure at least some scenes have heat, ache, or volatility of feeling

### Severity marker
- **Yellow:** bond exists, but voltage is low
- **Red:** the romance reads like companionship only

---

## 3. False conflict

### What it feels like
The story has conflict, but the conflict does not feel earned, necessary, or intelligent.

### Common symptoms
- arguments happen because the plot needs delay
- both characters avoid obvious truths with no real reason
- one overheard line causes impossible damage
- simple conversation would solve everything
- conflict is prolonged artificially
- the reader becomes impatient rather than invested

### Likely causes
- no real fault line has been built
- the writer is substituting withholding for structure
- fear responses are not psychologically grounded
- external pressure is too weak, so fake misunderstanding is doing the work
- the characters are being made dumber to preserve plot

### Diagnostic questions
- Why can they not resolve this honestly right now?
- What real fear, history, or pressure blocks resolution?
- Is the conflict proportionate to the relationship stakes?
- Does the problem reveal character, or merely stall plot?

### Repair actions
- replace fake misunderstanding with real incompatibility, fear, duty, shame, or consequence
- seed the fault line earlier
- strengthen external pressure
- let characters speak more directly where appropriate
- cut the manufactured argument and rebuild from the real problem

### Severity marker
- **Yellow:** one conflict beat is weak
- **Red:** the whole romance relies on fake blockage

---

## 4. Dead middle

### What it feels like
The manuscript loses force in the centre.

### Common symptoms
- several chapters feel interchangeable
- attraction loops repeat
- scenes feel pleasant but unnecessary
- nothing escalates
- the midpoint does not change the story enough
- the draft drifts instead of tightening

### Likely causes
- chapter jobs are not distinct
- no clear midpoint lock
- too many scenes of almost the same emotional temperature
- care, intimacy, and fear are not staged progressively
- the story is delaying the hard turn

### Diagnostic questions
- What changes at the midpoint?
- Does each middle chapter add new truth, new pressure, or new consequence?
- Which chapters could be swapped without anyone noticing?
- Are the pair closer in a different way, or merely still circling?

### Repair actions
- assign one clear job to each middle chapter
- strengthen midpoint change
- cut duplicate scenes
- add new forms of pressure after intimacy increases
- ensure the middle contains both deepening and destabilisation

### Severity marker
- **Yellow:** 1–2 soggy chapters
- **Red:** Act II has no real progression

---

## 5. Unearned rupture

### What it feels like
The breakup or major fracture arrives, but it does not land as inevitable or proportionate.

### Common symptoms
- the rupture feels too sudden
- the pair do not seem bonded enough for the breakup to hurt
- the cause feels petty or artificial
- the emotional blast is bigger than the setup supports
- the split is technically present but emotionally empty

### Likely causes
- the bond was not built strongly enough first
- the fault line was not seeded
- the breakup reason is fake or small
- fear response comes out of nowhere
- the writer wanted a genre beat, not a true fracture

### Diagnostic questions
- What exact fault line breaks them?
- Where was it visible earlier?
- Why is this break unavoidable at this moment?
- What does each person believe immediately after the rupture?

### Repair actions
- strengthen the bond before the rupture
- seed the breaking point earlier
- make the breakup come from a real moral, emotional, or practical collision
- ensure the rupture is specific to these characters
- cut theatrical drama that has no structural basis

### Severity marker
- **Yellow:** breakup is underprepared
- **Red:** breakup collapses under scrutiny

---

## 6. Weak repair

### What it feels like
The manuscript knows it must repair the relationship, but the repair is thin, rushed, or unconvincing.

### Common symptoms
- apology is vague
- one grand gesture replaces real understanding
- changed behaviour is absent
- the injured person forgives too fast
- the repair begins too late and has no breathing room
- the reunion feels administratively required

### Likely causes
- the writer fears slowing down for real repair
- the rupture was too large for the available page space
- the characters have not learned anything specific
- the story treats apology as enough
- reconciliation happens by declaration instead of action

### Diagnostic questions
- What is understood now that was not understood before?
- What specific harm is named?
- What changes in behaviour?
- Why should the injured person trust this repair?
- Is there enough page space for repair to feel real?

### Repair actions
- add a reflection chapter
- make apology specific
- add visible changed behaviour
- reduce rupture scale if the manuscript cannot properly repair it
- give the other person agency in deciding whether repair is accepted

### Severity marker
- **Yellow:** repair is present but too thin
- **Red:** reunion is unearned

---

## 7. Soft ending

### What it feels like
The book ends, but the ending does not settle strongly.

### Common symptoms
- confession happens, but future shape is unclear
- emotional closure is weak
- the final chapter says they are together but does not prove stability
- the last pages feel abrupt
- the reader leaves without a sense of earned rest or direction

### Likely causes
- no new stable pattern is shown
- final choice is emotional but not practical
- the ending arrives before the actual core problem is answered
- there is no calm payoff scene after the high point
- the story confuses union with resolution

### Diagnostic questions
- What does the relationship look like now in lived practice?
- What problem has actually been solved?
- What remains hard but manageable?
- Why does the reader believe this can hold?

### Repair actions
- add a settled ending scene
- show daily or practical proof of the new pattern
- clarify the terms of the relationship
- ensure the emotional and structural arc both close
- strengthen the last image or final exchange

### Severity marker
- **Yellow:** ending lands but lightly
- **Red:** ending is emotionally unconvincing

---

## 8. Point-of-view fog

### What it feels like
The romance is difficult to track because interiority, motive, or scene focus is muddy.

### Common symptoms
- the reader cannot tell who is driving the scene
- emotional turns feel blurry
- both characters sound too similar
- scene meaning depends on guesswork
- chapters contain too much internal noise and not enough directional clarity

### Likely causes
- point of view not disciplined
- emotional interpretation too abstract
- the prose is obscuring rather than clarifying
- subtext is treated as a replacement for legibility
- the draft is over-relying on implication

### Diagnostic questions
- Whose need shapes the scene?
- Can the reader state what each person wants?
- Does each point of view sound and process differently?
- Is the scene emotionally summarise-able?

### Repair actions
- stabilise point of view
- reduce abstract emotional language
- make wants and fears more explicit on the page
- strengthen transitions in scene turns
- cut decorative vagueness

### Severity marker
- **Yellow:** occasional muddiness
- **Red:** relationship logic is unreadable

---

## 9. ND falseness

### What it feels like
The draft claims ND truth, but the behaviour, sensory life, or communication patterns do not feel convincingly lived or consistent.

### Common symptoms
- ND traits appear only as labels
- overload appears when convenient, not as part of actual life
- characters seem written as “cute awkward”
- directness is flattened into bluntness only
- sensory or processing reality has no real effect on scenes
- the romance does not adapt around actual needs

### Likely causes
- diagnosis traits are being used decoratively
- the characters’ operating patterns are not integrated into plot or intimacy
- care does not reflect real regulation or communication needs
- the manuscript uses generic ND shorthand

### Diagnostic questions
- How does each person actually process stress, attraction, conflict, and recovery?
- What environments help or hinder them?
- How is care shaped by their needs?
- Where does the relationship become more possible because of accurate adaptation?

### Repair actions
- rewrite scenes to include real processing consequences
- make sensory reality continuous rather than occasional
- replace stereotype behaviour with specific behavioural patterns
- show love through accommodation, clarity, and accurate noticing
- ensure ND truth shapes the relationship form, not just character description

### Severity marker
- **Yellow:** under-integrated ND layer
- **Red:** ND framing feels cosmetic or false

---

## 10. Repetition without progression

### What it feels like
The draft keeps returning to similar scenes or emotions without forward motion.

### Common symptoms
- repeated almost-confessions
- repeated attraction beats
- repeated arguments with slightly different wording
- repeated “will they / will they not” without new stakes
- same scene energy across several chapters

### Likely causes
- chapter jobs not differentiated
- writer is buying time instead of advancing structure
- emotional progression not mapped
- no consequence chain
- fear response repeating without alteration

### Diagnostic questions
- What new thing does this chapter add?
- What is different now from the last similar scene?
- Has trust, risk, clarity, or damage changed?
- If the scene vanished, what would be lost?

### Repair actions
- cut duplicate scenes
- merge near-identical chapters
- assign clearer progression markers
- force each chapter to add new truth, new pressure, or new consequence
- use the beat tracker to test for structural sameness

### Severity marker
- **Yellow:** isolated repetition
- **Red:** the draft is structurally circling

# Scope decision

## Scene-level failure
The manuscript mostly works, but one or two scenes are weak.

### Fix by
- rewriting the scene purpose
- sharpening tension
- clarifying motive
- adding stronger scene turns
- improving care or bond evidence

## Chapter-level failure
A chapter or cluster of chapters is not doing the right job.

### Fix by
- changing chapter function
- cutting repeated material
- moving beats
- strengthening chapter-end shifts

## Act-level failure
A whole section of the manuscript is misbuilt.

### Fix by
- remapping Act I, II, or III
- changing midpoint logic
- rebuilding rupture or repair timing
- reducing structural drag

## Whole-book failure
The romance engine itself is not functioning.

### Fix by
- redefining the central relationship
- rebuilding the beat spine
- changing the fault line
- rethinking what the story is actually about

---

# Severity test

## Yellow
The draft is weakened, but the core engine still works.

Signs:
- the relationship is basically believable
- at least some scenes land
- the problem is repairable without rebuilding the whole structure

## Red
The draft’s main emotional promise is not yet working.

Signs:
- the central couple does not land
- the key genre beats feel fake
- the manuscript cannot be fixed by line editing alone
- structure must change

---

# Best repair types

## Add
Use when the draft is missing necessary evidence.

Add:
- care scenes
- correct noticing
- midpoint consequence
- reflection chapter
- settled ending proof

## Cut
Use when the draft is clogged with repetition.

Cut:
- duplicate attraction beats
- repeated arguments
- scenes with no outcome shift
- decorative side material weakening the spine

## Move
Use when the content is good but badly placed.

Move:
- reveal earlier
- pressure sooner
- rupture later or better seeded
- repair earlier with more room

## Rebuild
Use when the current version rests on a false premise.

Rebuild:
- the central conflict
- the breakup cause
- the repair path
- the relationship logic itself

# Whole-draft audit questions

## Bond audit
- Is the connection specific?
- Is care visible?
- Are they changed by knowing each other?

## Chemistry audit
- Is there charge?
- Is there desire, ache, tension, or relief?
- Is attraction more than generic approval?

## Conflict audit
- Is the conflict real?
- Does it arise from character, pressure, or consequence?
- Is it stronger than a simple conversation fix?

## Midpoint audit
- What changes permanently at the midpoint?
- Does the book get riskier after that point?

## Rupture audit
- Is the break both painful and intelligible?
- Was it earned?

## Repair audit
- What was learned?
- What changed?
- Why should reconciliation hold?

## Ending audit
- Does the ending prove a new pattern?
- Does it feel emotionally and structurally settled?

## ND truth audit
- Are sensory and processing realities shaping behaviour consistently?
- Is care adapted to actual need?
- Does the romance become more possible through mutual understanding?
